1.Introduction: Problem Set-up of Virtual Screening

  • For a given protein linked to a certain disease, we want to select a few small molecules (i.e., ligand) from a library of millions candidates such that the selected candidate will have the highest utility in disease treating.
  • For modern libraries, it is feasible scaling up to billions or even trillions of compounds enhances the reach and impact of virtual screening.

1.Introduction: Problem Set-up of Virtual Screening

Virtual screening presents several challenges:

  1. Multiple, competing objectives based on unknown, hard-to-quantify expert knowledge.
  2. Limited budget to try all ligands from the library.
  3. Some objectives (such as binding affinity) are expensive to evaluate even for a single ligand.

Our research aims to address these challenges:

  1. Actively eliciting expert preferences for virtual screening with many objectives.
  2. Active Virtual Screening.
  3. Neural Search Engine with diffusion model.

2.Eliciting Expert Preferences in Virtual Screening

  • Depending on the specific disease and protein, experts have preferences about characteristics of candidate ligands, trading off various criteria such as synthesizability, affinity, solubility, and side effects.
  • Some characteristics are “must-have,” such as synthesizability, while others are “nice-to-have.

2.Eliciting Expert Preferences in Virtual Screening

  • These implicit expert knowledge, encoded as preferences over ligands, are valuable to elicit for effective virtual screening. We can leverage toolkits from the field of machine learning from human preferences to tackle this challenge.

2.Eliciting Expert Preferences in Virtual Screening

Eliciting preference can be viewed as a logistic regression:

  • \( p(y \mid x_1, x_2) = \sigma(f(x_1) - f(x_2)) \) where \( \text{sigmoid}(z) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-z}} \).

If we assume utility is a linear function of ligand features, we can use gradient descent to achieve:

  • Train accuracy: 0.95
  • Test accuracy: 0.95

Alternatively, using a Gaussian process yields the same results:

  • Train accuracy: 0.95
  • Test accuracy: 0.95

2.Eliciting Expert Preferences in Virtual Screening

  • Initial Selection: Randomly select pairs of ligands from a library \( L \) of 7,000 compounds to explore the chemical space.
  • Adaptive Selection: Optimize future selections from \( L \) to reduce oracle queries, improving efficiency and estimating the weight vector \( W \).
  • Reward Function: For utility \( U \) using logistic regression \( U = W \cdot X \), where:
    • \( U \): utility score indicating ligand suitability
    • \( W \): weights representing feature importance
    • \( X \): ligand feature vector \(f(x_1) - f(x_2) \)

3. Active Virtual Screening

Even with the right trade-off objective elicited from expert, exhaustively screening millions of candidate from the virtual screening library is practically infeasible.

To address this problem, we can choose to screen ligand that looks promising, while avoid ligand that are highly certain to be a bad candidate.

  • Bayesian Optimization: Efficiently explores high-potential ligands.
  • Surrogate model (selection model): Gaussian Process, Neural net, Random forest
  • Acquisition function: UCB, Greedy, Thompson sampling

4. Neural Search Engine

Traditional physics-based docking tools (e.g., Glide, Smina) are computationally expensive to evaluate ligand affinity.
  • Traditional Tools: 15mins/1 pose
  • Our model: 5s/64 poses
  • Chai: 1 min/5 poses

We leverage the similarity in ligand structure to accelerate the binding by training a diffusion model for molecular docking
  • Neural Engine: Uses diffusion model for rapid docking
  • Utility Scoring: Assesses ligands on affinity, solubility, and toxicity, etc

5. Diffusion Model: Data collection

Data Augmentation Techniques:

  • Molecular Dynamics (MD) Augmentation: Employed 59,330 dynamic frames of 14,387 protein-ligand complexes to model ligand flexibility, amounting to 75K training data.
  • Data Crawling: Curated 322K protein-ligand complexes from the PDBScan22 dataset, after filtering out unnatural ligands and problematic poses, to enhance structural diversity.
  • Pharmacophore Alignment: Generated up to 11M pharmacophore-consistent ligand pairs from the Papyrus dataset, significantly expanding the model's training data.

5. Diffusion Model: Training

Diffusion models are probabilistic generative models that transform data from a noisy distribution into a structured, meaningful output through a reverse process.

Applying SDEs for controlled generative modeling:
  • Initiate with Gaussian noise on ligand coordinates
  • Employ Euler-Maruyama to denoise step-by-step:
    • \(X_{t-\Delta t} = X_t + f(X_t, t) \Delta t + g(t) \sqrt{\Delta t} \cdot \epsilon\)
  • Optimize ligand structure discovery through iterative sampling steps via Euler

5. Diffusion Model: Results

Benchmark on Posebusters Dataset:

Posebusters Version 1 includes 428 protein-ligand structures, and Version 2 has 308, all released to the PDB after 2021. Performance is measured by the percentage of protein-ligand pairs with pocket-aligned ligand RMSD under 2 Å.

7. Putting it all together

We perform active virtual screening on the inferred expert utility function. Our procedure respects expert preference (both hard and soft constraint) and probabilistic model to come up with a good candidate set. To search for poses required for objectives such as affinity, we accelerate the pose search by a neural search engine.

Metrics for evaluation

Regret
  • Definition: Difference in affinity between the best possible ligand and the top ligand found by the model within the \( \text{top}_k \% \).
  • \(\mathrm{Regret} = A_{\mathrm{best}} - A_{\mathrm{model}} \)

Percent of Best Ligand Found
  • Definition: Percentage of screened ligands close in affinity to the best possible ligand. (\(\text{top}_k \% \))
